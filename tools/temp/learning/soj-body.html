Joy can help you

feel better.

When you're feeling joyful, your body is better equipped to fend off illness and disease, thanks to improved immune function and lower inflammation. [2, 3]

live longer.

According to research, joy may even add extra years to your lifespan. [4]

stress less.

A burst of joy can help your body return to a state of calm and balance, lowering cortisol, blood pressure, and heart rate. [1]

stop hitting snooze.

Studies show that feeling joyful can help you sleep better at night. [2]

finish your to-do’s.

Feeling joy can make you up to 12% more productive. That means saving almost a full hour every workday. [5]

make better decisions.

Studies show that business leaders make better decisions when feeling joyful, and joyful doctors come to a correct diagnosis more quickly. (One reason? People consider more scenarios in the decision-making process, and are less likely to get hung up on the wrong choices.) [9, 10]

find common ground.

Joyful negotiators reach more win-win agreements. [6]

keep the spark alive.

Sharing moments of joy with a partner deepens the sense of intimacy and trust in the relationship. [7]

be a better BFF.

Sharing in a friend's joy strengthens your bond. When you celebrate their good news enthusiastically, it deepens the friendship. [8]

think outside the box.

Bursts of joy are linked to bursts of creativity, both for individuals and within organizations. Joy increases cognitive flexibility, which is key to innovative thinking. [11, 12]

be a kickass boss.

Joyful leaders inspire excellence in their teams. When leaders are feeling good, their teams not only perform better, but waste less effort in getting their work done. [11]

turn heads.

Studies show that a genuine, joyful smile makes the average person more physically attractive. (More attractive, in some cases, than a model who doesn’t smile.) [14]

close the sale.

A joyful salesperson means more time spent in the store, higher ratings, and a greater chance of repeat business. (More attractive, in some cases, than a model who doesn’t smile.) [14]

get out of a rut.

Joy broadens your mindset, making you more exploratory and open to new possibilities. [13]

work together.

Research shows that when you’re feeling joyful, you're more likely to lend a helping hand, collaborate, and communicate more effectively. [15]

I’m Ingrid Fetell Lee, designer, author, and founder of the School of Joy.

Fifteen years ago, I set out to understand why certain places and things bring us joy. That research has since become a blog, a TED talk, and a bestselling book called Joyful.

And while I never pictured myself at the helm of a happiness school, it makes a strange kind of sense.

Science says that joy has a powerful, transformative effect on our well-being.

Design says, “Let’s make more of it.”

And that’s exactly what we do here. We take peer-reviewed papers and turn them into practices that help you design a better life.

Joy isn’t a nice-to-have. It’s a must-have. And we’re here to help you find it.

Joy is not a luxury.
